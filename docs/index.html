Skiing into climate crises

Why two out of three ski resorts in austria will have to close in the coming years.

By Lisa Lind

Austria is a ski nation par excellence - not only in professional sports but also in popular sports. Three out of four people in Austria ski or snowboard, and many of them learn it from their parents, as this study commissioned by a major sports retailer shows.

But the numbers are decreasing. Fewer and fewer Austrians are actually going skiing. And there are two reasons for this: skiing is becoming more and more expensive and there is less and less snow. Small ski resorts at low altitudes in particular are struggling to survive, yet it is precisely these resorts where many children learn to ski, because they are cheaper and often close to home and therefore easier to reach.

Although Austria is located in the Alps, there are many ski resorts at lower altitudes. And this is increasingly becoming a problem. Small ski resorts are particularly affected.

And many of them also situated in a low altitude. Climate researchers at the Climate Change Center Austria, CCCA , assume that ski resorts above 2,000m sea level will continue to have natural snow. In ski areas above 1,500m, artificial snow could be used to help - but only if the earth warms by a maximum of 2 degrees. Current calculations by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change IPCC, however, assume that we are heading for a plus of 3.2 degrees, which would also jeopardize snowmaking in many areas around 2,000m. This means that at least 50 percent of all ski resorts in Austria are at acute risk.

How does the ski indsutrie react?.

Over 6 percent of Austria's GDP is generated by tourism, and 300,000 jobs are directly or indirectly linked to tourism. Skiing and winter tourism have always been dominated by the large ski resorts in the west of the country.

They are located in the middle of the Alps, tend to be higher and therefore have fewer problems with a lack of snow. And so it comes as no surprise when the powerful head president of the Austrian Ski Association ÖSV questions man-made climate change in a TV-interview.

At the same time, the major ski resorts are also experiencing the effects of the climate crisis. More and more slopes have to be widened because there is a lack of snow even at high altitudes and space is needed for snow cannons and snow groomers. As a result, even entire mountain peaks are being blown up to make room.

Here on the Pitztal Glacier, part of the mountain was blown up in a nature reserve in 2018 to widen the slope. There was no permit for this .

Depending on how hard the climate crisis is tackled, the forecasts look better or worse. But one thing is already certain: by 2050 there will be no more glaciers in Austria - this development can no longer be stopped.
